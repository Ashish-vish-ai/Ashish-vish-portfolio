AR/AP Dashboard

An internal financial tool designed to help finance teams monitor accounts receivable and payable in real time. It surfaces outstanding amounts, salesperson-level exposure, and payment behavior — giving the people responsible for financial health a clear picture of where things stand, without digging through spreadsheets.

What I Did

Designed a data-heavy dashboard from scratch — which meant understanding the finance domain first, then deciding how to structure and visualize it.

Information Architecture

Structured complex financial data into a clear, scannable hierarchy — surfacing key metrics first, detailed data behind further interaction.

Data Visualization Design

Designed charts and summary views to help users quickly understand receivables, payment delays, and salesperson performance patterns.

Domain Understanding

Learned core finance concepts — AR/AP cycles, invoice delays, exposure — and translated them into UI components that felt native to how finance teams think.

The Problem

Finance teams were operating without centralized visibility into the data they needed most — and compensating with manual effort.

01

No financial visibility

There was no centralized view of AR and AP — teams had to piece together exposure from multiple fragmented sources.

02

Blind to sales-level impact

No easy way to see which salespeople were driving outstanding receivables — making accountability and follow-up difficult.

03

Manual reporting effort

Generating reports and analyzing payment delays required manual work across fragmented data sources every time.

Key Decisions

The specific calls I made — and why I made them.

01
Information Hierarchy

Summary metrics before detailed data views

Finance users need quick answers before deep analysis. A dashboard that leads with raw tables forces users to do mental arithmetic before they understand the situation. By surfacing high-level metrics first — total receivables, delays, distribution — I ensured users could immediately gauge financial health, then drill into specifics when they needed to.

02
Data Visualization

Visual patterns for delays and trends over raw tables

Raw financial data — rows of invoice amounts and dates — makes pattern recognition nearly impossible at scale. By representing trends like invoice delays and payment behavior as charts, users could identify outliers and problems in seconds rather than minutes. The visualization wasn't decorative; it was the primary interface for understanding the data.

03
Usability

Report exports integrated into the dashboard, not a separate tool

Finance teams frequently need to share data externally — with leadership, auditors, or external partners. Building export functionality directly into the dashboard removed a friction point that would otherwise push users back to manual tools. If the data is already structured correctly in the dashboard, the export should be one click away.

Outcome

The dashboard gave finance teams a centralized view of their accounts receivable and payable — replacing fragmented manual processes with a single interface for visibility, analysis, and reporting. Delays became visible at a glance. Salesperson impact was trackable. Reports were a click away.

This project pushed me outside familiar UX territory and into analytical, data-heavy design. Understanding the finance domain well enough to design for it — learning what AR/AP cycles mean, why invoice delays matter, how salesperson exposure affects the business — was as much of the work as the design itself. That shift, from designing interfaces to designing for how people think about complex systems, is something I carry into every project now.
