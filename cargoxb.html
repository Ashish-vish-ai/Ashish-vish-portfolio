CargoXB

A freight rate aggregation platform for freight forwarders. Instead of visiting five carrier websites and re-entering the same shipment details each time, users input their requirements once and get all available rates — consolidated, comparable, actionable — in a single interface.

What I Did

Sole designer on the project. I owned the process end-to-end — from understanding the domain to delivering production-ready screens.

Research & Audit

Studied existing freight platforms to understand domain patterns, information hierarchy, and what data freight forwarders actually need to make a decision.

End-to-End Flows

Mapped and designed every user flow — from sign-up through shipment input, rate discovery, comparison, and booking.

UI Design

Designed all major screens and a consistent component library for a complex logistics product — independently, without a senior designer to reference.

The Problem

Freight forwarders were stuck in a fragmented, repetitive workflow for every single booking — no matter how routine.

01

One platform at a time

Checking Maersk, ONE, Hapag-Lloyd — each required a separate visit, a separate login, a separate flow.

02

Repeated data entry

Origin, destination, load type, weight, dimensions — re-entered from scratch on every carrier platform.

03

No real comparison

Rates came back in inconsistent formats. Comparing them meant context-switching and mental arithmetic.

Key Decisions

The specific calls I made — and why I made them.

01
Input Design

Step-by-step flow instead of one long form

Freight booking genuinely requires a lot of fields: origin, destination, load type, FCL vs LCL, dimensions, weight, category. Putting all of them on one screen would make the task feel enormous. Breaking it into 4 progressive steps made each stage feel achievable and reduced the chance of errors — users could focus on one decision at a time.

02
Results Layout

Comparison-first rate listing over a detail-first view

The user's primary job on the results page isn't to study one carrier — it's to find the best option across many. I designed the list so all the decision-relevant information (carrier, price, transit time) was visible at a glance, in a consistent format row by row. The detail view was a second step, not the default.

03
Visual Direction

Clarity and density over visual complexity

Early explorations leaned into branded, designed-looking UI — gradients, cards with heavy shadows, illustrative elements. I pulled back. Freight forwarders are professionals using this tool to do a job. What builds trust is legible data and a predictable interface — not visual polish. The final design is deliberately functional.

Outcome

Delivered a complete, end-to-end design for a complex B2B logistics product — working independently as the only designer, in a domain I had no prior experience in. The product covered sign-up, shipment input, rate comparison, and booking selection.

More than the deliverables, this project taught me how to design without a safety net. No senior designer to ask, no established component library, no prior user research to build on. I had to trust research, trust iteration, and trust my own judgement — which is exactly the muscle product design requires.
